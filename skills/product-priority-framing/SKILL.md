# Product Priority Framing

Turn validated discovery, product feedback, delivery learnings, and product constraints into a concrete Product Owner package that downstream solutioning, planning, and approval consumers can use without reconstructing product intent or tradeoffs from scattered notes.

For this repository, this is the preferred direct-support skill for the Product Owner role when the work needs explicit product-direction and priority-framing discipline rather than only general grounding or handoff packaging.

## Quick start

1. Read the validated discovery, feedback, and delivery context together.
2. Define the actual product decision boundary and time horizon.
3. Make product value, tradeoffs, deferred scope, and acceptance guardrails explicit.
4. Distinguish product-priority readiness from implementation or release commitment.
5. Make reprioritization and re-review triggers explicit so downstream consumers know when the current package no longer applies.

## Workflow

### 1) Read the product context

- Start with validated requirements, business objectives, process analysis, feedback signals, release learnings, verification findings, and relevant specifications.
- Pull in roadmap notes, KPI reviews, support trends, or stakeholder notes only as needed to understand the current product choice honestly.
- Keep decision limits explicit instead of silently expanding the product scope.

### 2) Frame the product direction clearly

- State what product area, initiative set, horizon, user segment, or outcome focus is actually in scope.
- Distinguish intended scope, excluded scope, and explicitly deferred scope.
- Preserve value hypotheses, customer or business need, constraints, and trust-boundary-sensitive product concerns that materially shape the direction.

### 3) Author the product-priority posture cleanly

For the current product package, capture:

- the product goal or outcome focus
- the validated needs, evidence, and signals that currently support the direction
- what work is prioritized next and why
- the tradeoffs, tensions, and non-goals that shaped the ordering
- whether the package is ready for downstream shaping, ready with conditions, or blocked
- any scope, timing, evidence, or approval limits on that posture
- whether further planning, implementation commitment, or release approval is still required downstream

### 4) Preserve acceptance guardrails without overclaiming

- Record the must-have outcomes, user-facing guardrails, and behavior-sensitive conditions that downstream roles should preserve.
- Keep product-level acceptance guidance explicit rather than vague.
- Do not present product-level acceptance framing as if it were already a full behavioral specification, architecture package, or verification plan.

### 5) Make re-review and reprioritization conditions explicit

- State what feedback changes, evidence updates, release learnings, timing changes, or business shifts should trigger another product pass.
- Do not let "priority" language silently extend beyond the reviewed horizon, scope, or current evidence state.
- Preserve separation between product direction, technical design, execution planning, and operational approval.

## Guardrails

- Do not treat stakeholder pressure as a substitute for accountable product judgment.
- Do not present prioritized work as already committed for implementation, sprint intake, or release when downstream authorization is still required.
- Do not present product-level acceptance framing as a substitute for full behavioral specification or verification planning.
- Do not hide deferred scope, weak evidence, or unresolved tensions behind confident roadmap language.
- Do not let product scope, decision horizon, or tradeoff logic stay implicit.

## Notes

- This skill is intentionally narrow. It supports Product Owner direction and prioritization work after validated discovery already exists.
- It is not a discovery skill, not a technical-design skill, not a sprint-administration method, and not a release-approval override.
